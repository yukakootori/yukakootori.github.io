Teaching

Courses in U.S. history, migration, and the history of childhood.

Teaching Philosophy

I believe that the study of history cultivates two indispensable capacities: the ability to hold complexity without forcing premature resolution, and the ability to recognize that what seems natural or inevitable has always been made—and can therefore be remade.

My seminars are organized around primary source analysis and collaborative inquiry. I ask students to sit with the difficulty of historical evidence—its silences, its contradictions, its distortions—before reaching for interpretation. I am particularly interested in how students who come from outside the United States read American history, and I design my courses to put that outsider perspective to work analytically.
