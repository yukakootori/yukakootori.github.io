Research

Migration history, childhood, and the making of the U.S. border state.

Book Project

Disposable Childhoods: Child Migration and the Guardianship Principle at the U.S. Border, 1890s–1920s

In Progress

This book examines how the concept of guardianship—the idea that the state, like a parent, bore protective obligations toward vulnerable children—was mobilized, contested, and ultimately transformed by the crisis of child migration at U.S. land borders in the Progressive Era.

Drawing on immigration case files, consular correspondence, and the records of child welfare organizations, Disposable Childhoods traces the careers of children apprehended at the U.S.–Mexico and U.S.–Canada borders between the 1890s and the 1920s: children traveling alone, children used as decoys by smugglers, children whose nationalities were unclear, and children whose families could not be found. It asks how officials decided which children deserved protection and which deserved deportation, and how those decisions encoded racial, gender, and class hierarchies into the administrative infrastructure of border control.

The project argues that the guardianship principle was not simply a humanitarian counterweight to exclusion but a technology of governance—one that sorted children into categories of belonging and disposability that prefigure contemporary debates about “unaccompanied alien children.”

Archival Research

My research draws on records held at the National Archives and Records Administration (College Park, MD and Washington, D.C.), the Library of Congress, the National Archives of the United Kingdom, and archives in Mexico City and Geneva. I have also conducted research at state and municipal archives in California, Texas, Arizona, and New York.
