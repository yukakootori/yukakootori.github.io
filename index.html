Yukako Otori
大鳥 由香子

Associate Professor · Keio University

I am a historian of migration, childhood, and U.S. border governance in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. My work explores how ideas about child welfare, guardianship, and national belonging shaped the administration of borders—and the lives of children caught at their edges.

My current book project, Disposable Childhoods, examines child migration and the guardianship principle at the U.S.–Mexico and U.S.–Canada borders from the 1890s through the 1920s. I hold a Ph.D. from Harvard University and have held fellowships at Harvard’s Center for European Studies and the Graduate Institute (HION) in Geneva.

About

My research sits at the intersection of U.S. immigration history, the history of childhood, and the history of the state. I am particularly interested in how administrative categories —the “unaccompanied child,” the “ward of the state,” the “alien minor”—were invented and contested in the Progressive Era, and how those categories continue to shape contemporary border policy.

Before joining Keio, I taught at Harvard University (2015–2018), Hosei University (2021–2023), and Tokyo University of Foreign Studies (2020–2024). I am an affiliate of the Child Labour and Poverty Network (CLPN) at Queen Mary University of London, a Harvard Center for European Studies Fellow, and a member of the History of International Organizations Network (HION) at the Graduate Institute Geneva.
